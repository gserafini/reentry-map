# Geographic Expansion Strategy
## Reentry Map: Prioritized National Coverage Plan

**Last Updated**: 2025-11-08
**Purpose**: Data-driven geographic expansion plan for systematic national coverage based on returning citizen populations
**Note**: This research will inform the design and implementation of the "Request for Resources" API

---

## Executive Summary

This document provides a prioritized roadmap for expanding Reentry Map's coverage across the United States, based on absolute returning citizen populations, release volumes, and market opportunities.

**Key Findings**:
- **~600,000** people released from state/federal prisons annually
- **~7.2 million** people cycle through local jails annually
- **~19 million** Americans living with felony convictions (potential user base)
- **5 states** (CA, FL, IL, NY, TX) account for majority of prison releases
- Urban areas disproportionately impacted: **90%** of formerly incarcerated individuals lived in urban zip codes before incarceration
- Geographic concentration: In major cities, **50-70%** of returning citizens concentrate in just **7-10%** of neighborhoods

**Strategic Approach**:
1. **Phase 1**: California expansion (capitalize on existing infrastructure)
2. **Phase 2**: Top 5 release states (CA, FL, IL, NY, TX)
3. **Phase 3**: Major metropolitan areas with concentrated populations
4. **Phase 4**: Regional coverage by census region
5. **Phase 5**: Comprehensive national coverage

## Data Limitations & Methodology

### Challenge: Lack of City-Level Data

**Critical Gap**: The Bureau of Justice Statistics (BJS) and other federal agencies primarily collect prison release data at the **state level**, not city level. City-specific release statistics are not published in regular annual reports.

**Implications**:
- Cannot rank cities by exact release volumes
- Must infer priorities from:
  - State-level release data
  - Metropolitan area incarceration rates
  - Urban concentration patterns
  - Known reentry program locations
  - Population size and demographics

### Research Sources

Our analysis draws from:
- **Bureau of Justice Statistics** (BJS) - State prison releases, national trends
- **Prison Policy Initiative** - Urban incarceration rates, geographic analysis
- **Vera Institute** - Metropolitan area data, jail populations
- **Urban Institute** - City-specific reentry studies
- **U.S. Census Bureau** - Incarcerated population origins

### Methodology

**Prioritization Criteria** (in order):
1. **Absolute returning citizen population** (state-level releases)
2. **Metropolitan statistical area** (MSA) size and density
3. **Known geographic concentration** (research-documented patterns)
4. **Existing reentry infrastructure** (indicates demand + resources)
5. **Market adjacency** (expand from existing coverage)
6. **Data availability** (211 directories, government databases)

## National Overview: Key Statistics

### Annual Release Volumes

**Prisons** (2022 data, most recent available):
- **443,534** releases from state prisons (excluding deaths)
- **~55,000** federal prison releases
- **Total: ~600,000** prison releases annually

**Jails** (2022 data):
- **7,215,958** releases from local jails annually
- Caveat: Many individuals cycle through jails multiple times per year

### Total Population Under Correctional Control

- **~1.9 million** people in prisons and jails (2023)
- **~19 million** people living with felony convictions
- **46%** of U.S. adults have an immediate family member who has been incarcerated

### Geographic Concentration Patterns

**Urban Concentration**:
- **90%** of incarcerated individuals lived in urban zip codes before incarceration
- **10%** lived in rural zip codes

**Neighborhood-Level Concentration** (documented in research):
- **New York City**: 72% of state prisoners came from just 7 of 55 community districts
- **Chicago**: 54% of male prisoners returned to just 7 of 77 neighborhoods
- **Boston**: 40% of reentry program participants returned to just 2 neighborhoods

**Implication**: Within major metros, target specific high-concentration neighborhoods first.

## Phase 5: Comprehensive National Coverage

### Small Metro & Rural Strategy

**Challenge**: 10% of incarcerated individuals lived in rural zip codes, but coverage is expensive (dispersed population).

**Approach**:
1. **State capital coverage**: Ensure every state capital has resources (government programs concentrated there)
2. **Prison town coverage**: Cities with major state prisons (prison towns often have reentry services)
3. **Regional hubs**: One city per region in each state (can serve surrounding counties)
4. **Rural aggregation**: County-level resources that serve multiple towns

**Priority rural/small metros**:
- Prison towns: San Quentin (CA), Attica (NY), Angola (LA), Florence (AZ)
- State capitals not yet covered: Trenton (NJ), Harrisburg (PA), Lansing (MI), etc.
- Regional hubs: Example - Fresno serves Central Valley, Spokane serves Eastern WA

### Territory & US Possession Strategy

**Covered eventually**:
- **Puerto Rico** (3.2M) - San Juan metro (2.4M) - Spanish language primary
- **US Virgin Islands** (100K)
- **Guam** (170K)

**Data limitation**: Federal BJS data often excludes territories; will require local partnerships.

## Request for Resources API: Implementation Strategy

### API Design Concept

The "Request for Resources" API will allow:
1. **Requesting resources** for specific cities/categories that don't yet have coverage
2. **Upvoting existing requests** to indicate demand
3. **Tracking progress** on requested locations
4. **Notifications** when requested coverage goes live

### API Request Structure

**Endpoint**: `POST /api/resource-requests`

```json
{
  "city": "Atlanta",
  "state": "GA",
  "categories": ["employment", "housing", "legal-aid"],
  "user_story": "I'm returning to Atlanta and need help finding job training programs",
  "estimated_users": 50,  // optional: how many people this would help
  "priority_reason": "Large metro, no current coverage"
}
```

### Prioritization Algorithm

Rank resource requests by:
1. **Vote count** (demand signal)
2. **City population** (absolute impact)
3. **State release volume** (need indicator)
4. **Geographic clustering** (efficiency - easier to do 3 TX cities than 3 scattered states)
5. **Data availability** (can we quickly populate it?)
6. **Community partner presence** (local reentry orgs to collaborate with)

### Request Processing Workflow

1. **User submits request** → Stored in `resource_requests` table
2. **Other users can upvote** → Increment `vote_count`
3. **Admin reviews monthly** → Prioritize based on algorithm
4. **Research phase** → If prioritized, assign to AI discovery agent or manual research
5. **Resource population** → Aim for 30+ resources minimum
6. **Launch notification** → Email all users who requested/upvoted

### Metrics to Track

- **Requests by city** (shows demand heat map)
- **Requests by category** (shows service gaps)
- **Time-to-fulfill** (request → launch)
- **User who requested is still active** when launched (retention indicator)

### Integration with Expansion Strategy

**Requested cities feed into this doc's priorities**:
- If 50+ users request "Atlanta", bump Atlanta from Phase 3A to earlier
- If many requests for specific category (e.g., "substance abuse in Phoenix"), prioritize that category during Phoenix research
- User demand validates or challenges our population-based assumptions

## Risks & Mitigation Strategies

### Risk 1: Data Quality at Scale

**Risk**: As we expand to 100+ cities, quality may degrade (outdated info, unverified resources)

**Mitigation**:
- AI verification agent runs quarterly on all resources
- Community reporting incentivized (gamification, badges)
- Partner with local reentry coalitions for each city (crowdsource verification)
- Minimum quality threshold before city launch (30+ resources, 80% verified)

### Risk 2: Regional Variation in Services

**Risk**: Service landscapes vary dramatically (CA has robust expungement programs, Southern states less so)

**Mitigation**:
- Category flexibility by region (don't force fit)
- Highlight service gaps transparently (turn absence into advocacy data)
- Partner with national orgs to create resources where gaps exist
- Use "no resources found" pages to drive advocacy ("Contact your representative")

### Risk 3: Funding Constraints

**Risk**: Expansion requires research time, may outpace funding

**Mitigation**:
- Prioritize high-ROI cities (large populations, existing infrastructure)
- AI discovery agent amortizes research costs
- Phased launch (don't need 100 resources day 1, start with 30 and grow)
- User-submitted resources reduce research burden
- Grant applications tied to specific phases (e.g., "Fund Phase 2A Texas expansion")

### Risk 4: Local Partnership Resistance

**Risk**: Local reentry orgs may view as competition or threat

**Mitigation**:
- Position as amplifier, not competitor (we drive traffic to them)
- Share user data insights (anonymized heat maps of need)
- Co-branding opportunities (e.g., "Powered by [Local Org]")
- Invite to be data stewards (update their own listings)
- Revenue sharing if monetize (e.g., % of grant funding)

### Risk 5: User Trust & Adoption

**Risk**: Population may distrust new tech platform, prefer word-of-mouth

**Mitigation**:
- Partner endorsements (reentry orgs, probation departments)
- Peer testimonials (formerly incarcerated success stories)
- Offline presence (QR codes at jails, probation offices, community centers)
- Word-of-mouth incentives (refer a friend, both get premium features)
- Transparent about data privacy (what we collect, what we don't, why)
